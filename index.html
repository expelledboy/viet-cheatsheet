Getting Started

▶

0. The Core Idea

Vietnamese is very learnable, but beginners need to understand a few things immediately:

  1. Tones matter
  2. Vowels matter
  3. Pronouns are social
  4. Spelling patterns are contextual
  5. Typing Vietnamese is easy once you know Telex
  6. Speaking imperfectly is better than waiting for perfection
Best beginner mindset:
Be understandable first, elegant later. Learn the high-frequency daily phrases first. Use Vietnamese often, even if your grammar is simple.

Sound System

▶

1. Phonetics & Tones

In Vietnamese, tone is part of the word. If you miss the tone, you may change the meaning completely.

The 6 Tones

Using ma as the classic example:

#NameExampleDescription
1ngangmaflat / mid
2sắcmárising
3huyềnmàfalling
4hỏimảdipping / questioning
5ngãmãhigh rising / broken
6nặngmạlow dropping / heavy
Remember: Tone changes meaning. Tone is not optional. Written marks matter.

Beginner note: Standard written Vietnamese distinguishes all 6 tones. In much southern speech, hỏi and ngã may sound similar or merge somewhat.

2. The Hard Vowels

These are the vowels English speakers often miss.

Core Vowel Distinctions

LetterSound
ăshort a; very brief "a"

ôclose, clipped oh

đnormal hard English-like d
d / gioften z in the North, often y in the South

Vowel Families

Learn these as contrast groups:

  • a / ă / â
  • o / ô / ơ
  • u / ư
  • e / ê
These are not decorative marks. They are different vowels.

3. Pronunciation Rules

Some Vietnamese letters are pronounced differently, or spelled differently, depending on what letters come next. This is one of the most important reading rules for English speakers.

3.1 c / k / qu

These usually represent roughly the same base k sound.

  • c before a, o, ô, ơ, u, ư
  • k before e, ê, i
  • q usually appears as qu

Examples: cá, kẹo, quay

Different spellings, similar base sound — often just orthographic context.

3.2 g / gh

Same base sound, spelling changes by following vowel.

  • g before a, o, ô, ơ, u, ư
  • gh before e, ê, i

Examples: ga, ghế, ghi

3.3 ng / ngh

Same ng sound (like the end of English "sing"), spelling changes by next vowel.

  • ng before a, o, ô, ơ, u, ư
  • ngh before e, ê, i

Examples: nga, nghe, nghỉ

3.4 gi

gi is usually one sound, not separate g + i.

  • Northern: often like z
  • Southern: often like y

Examples: gì, giỏi, giới

3.5 d vs đ

These are different letters.

  • đ = hard English-like d
  • d = often z (North), often y (South)

Examples: đi, dì

3.6 qu

Pronounced together, often roughly like kw or closer to w depending on dialect.

Examples: qua, quê, quên

3.7 nh

nh = soft ny sound. Similar in spirit to Spanish ñ.

Examples: nhà, nhanh

3.8 th

th in Vietnamese is not English "th." It is an aspirated t.

Examples: thôi, thích

3.9 ph

ph = f sound.

Examples: phở, phải

3.10 kh

kh = rough throaty sound, like German Bach or Scottish loch.

Examples: không, khỏe

3.11 tr and ch

Different sounds, though beginners often hear them as similar at first.

Examples: trà, cha

3.12 Tiny Rule

When you see extra letters such as the h in gh / ngh, they often do not create a brand-new English-style sound. They often exist to show which vowel can follow while preserving the same base sound.

3.13 Memorize as Spelling Families

  • c / k / qu
  • g / gh
  • ng / ngh
  • d / gi / đ
  • ph / th / kh / nh
This makes reading Vietnamese much easier, much faster.

Grammar Basics

▶

5. Pronouns

Pronouns in Vietnamese are not just grammar. They express relationship, age, respect, warmth, and social role. This is one of the biggest differences from English.

5.1 Safe Basics

  • tôi = I / me, neutral-polite
  • mình = I / me, softer, warmer, friendlier
  • bạn = you, neutral/friendly

5.2 The Sibling Rule

  • anh = older brother / you (male, older) / I (if male & older)
  • chị = older sister / you (female, older) / I (if female & older)
  • em = younger person / you / I in younger position

5.3 Elders & Older Adults

  • cô = aunt / older woman / teacher
  • chú = uncle / older man
  • bác = older aunt/uncle generation
  • ông = grandfather / elderly man
  • bà = grandmother / elderly woman

5.4 Very Safe Beginner Rule

If unsure, use tôi for "I" and bạn for "you." Then gradually learn family-style pronouns as you get more comfortable.

5.5 Social Note

Vietnamese often uses kinship terms where English would use "you" and "I." This is normal, warm, and culturally important.

6. Sentence Structure

Vietnamese basic word order is usually: Subject + Verb + Object

This is pleasantly familiar for English speakers.

  • Tôi ăn cơm. = I eat rice.
  • Tôi thích cà phê. = I like coffee.
  • Tôi học tiếng Việt. = I study Vietnamese.
  • Tôi không hiểu. = I do not understand.

6.1 Negation

  • không = not / no

6.2 Existence / Possession

  • có = have / there is / yes in some contexts
▶

7. Time, Aspect & Particles

Vietnamese does not use tense the way English does. Instead, it often uses time words and particles.

7.1 Core Particles

  • đã = past / already
  • đang = currently / in progress
  • sẽ = future / will
  • vừa = just happened
  • chưa = not yet
  • rồi = already / finished / then
  • nữa = more / again

7.2 Examples

  • Tôi đang học tiếng Việt. = I am learning Vietnamese.
  • Tôi đã ăn rồi. = I already ate.
  • Tôi sẽ đi. = I will go.
  • Tôi vừa đến. = I just arrived.
  • Tôi chưa hiểu. = I do not understand yet.

7.3 Polite & Softening Particles

  • ạ = respectful sentence ending
  • dạ = polite "yes" / polite response starter
  • nhé / nha = friendly, soft sentence ending

Examples: Dạ, em hiểu ạ. • Đi nhé. • Ngồi đây nha.

These do a lot of social work in Vietnamese.
▶

8. Classifiers

Vietnamese commonly uses classifiers with nouns.

Useful Beginner Classifiers

  • cái = general thing / object
  • con = animal
  • người = person
  • ly = glass / cup, especially drinks

Examples

  • một cái bàn = one table
  • một con mèo = one cat
  • một người bạn = one friend
  • một ly cà phê = one cup of coffee

18. Reactions & Slang

Useful to sound more natural and expressive.

  • Trời ơi! = Oh my God! / Oh wow!
  • Bó tay = I give up / what can you do
  • Xịn = high quality / awesome
  • Chất = cool / stylish / solid
  • Ủa? = Huh? / Wait, what?
  • Ngon quá! = So delicious!
  • Đẹp quá! = So beautiful!
  • Mệt quá! = So tired!
These are heard constantly in everyday speech.

11. Introductions

Useful self-introduction lines:

  • Tôi là ... = I am ...
  • Tôi tên là ... = My name is ...
  • Tôi đến từ ... = I come from ...
  • Tôi sống ở ... = I live in ...
  • Tôi nói tiếng Anh. = I speak English.
  • Tôi đang học tiếng Việt. = I am learning Vietnamese.
  • Rất vui được gặp bạn. = Nice to meet you.
You can make a full mini-introduction from only 3–4 of these.

12.1 Greetings

  • Xin chào. = Hello.
  • Chào bạn. = Hi.
  • Tạm biệt. = Goodbye.
  • Hẹn gặp lại. = See you again.

12.2 Thanks & Apology

  • Cảm ơn. = Thank you.
  • Cảm ơn nhiều. = Thanks a lot.
  • Không có gì. = You're welcome / no problem.
  • Xin lỗi. = Sorry / excuse me.

12.3 Confirmation

  • Vâng. = yes (polite)
  • Dạ. = polite yes / respectful response
  • Không. = no
  • Được. = okay / can / acceptable
  • Không sao. = it's okay / no problem

13. Social Language

13.1 The Classic Everyday Phrase

  • Ăn cơm chưa? = Have you eaten yet?
This can function not only as a literal food question, but as a warm social check-in similar to "How are you?" in certain everyday contexts.

13.2 Tone & Friendliness

Vietnamese daily speech often sounds relational, not purely informational. Warmth, small particles, and the right pronoun matter a lot.

Culture

▶

20. Cultural Etiquette

Social and cultural behaviors that help daily interaction go more smoothly.

20.1 Use Both Hands

When giving or receiving items, using two hands can feel more respectful.

20.2 Head & Feet

  • Avoid touching someone's head.
  • Avoid pointing your feet at people or sacred spaces.

20.3 Shoes Off

Remove shoes before entering a home, and often at temples or certain indoor spaces.

20.4 Dress Modestly at Religious Sites

Cover shoulders and knees when visiting temples or religious places.

20.5 Greet Elders First

Respect toward elders is important. Use calm, polite, warm speech.

20.6 General Demeanor

A calm, smiling, respectful manner goes a long way. Vietnamese communication often values warmth and social sensitivity.

25. Final Message

Vietnamese becomes much easier once you stop treating it like English with accents and start treating it like its own sound system.

Your first wins should be:

  • Hearing the tones
  • Noticing the vowel differences
  • Using the right pronoun roughly well
  • Typing with Telex
  • Saying short daily phrases confidently
You do not need perfect grammar to start speaking. You need repetition, listening, and willingness to speak.

Use Vietnamese every Friday at Chillax, use it in cafés, use it with friends, use it badly at first, and keep going.

That is how it starts to become natural.
